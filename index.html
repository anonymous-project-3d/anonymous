Foundation or Facade: Probing the Limits of Compositional Geometry in 3D Point Cloud Foundation Models

Abstract

Recent advances in large-scale pretraining have produced powerful 3D point cloud foundation models with strong performance on a variety of downstream benchmarks. Yet it remains unclear whether these models genuinely support compositional geometric reasoning, or whether their success is largely driven by object-centric pattern recognition. In this work, we examine the compositional geometric understanding of 3D point cloud representations through a diagnostic, modelagnostic probing framework. We posit that robust geometric awareness requires not only encoding the intrinsic geometry of individual objects, but also reasoning over inter-object spatial relationships and global scene layout. To operationalize this view, we probe frozen backbone representations across a suite of tasks, including affordance prediction, grasp pose estimation, semanticgeometry classiffcation, and absolute and relative 6D pose estimation, under both isolated-object and multi-object regimes. Across architectures and tasks, our experiments reveal a consistent pattern. While large pretrained encoders perform strongly on isolated-object geometry, their performance degrades substantially in compositional settings, often more severely than that of classical geometrically biased architectures. These findings suggest that, despite recent progress, existing 3D foundation models may not yet robustly support compositional geometric understanding.
